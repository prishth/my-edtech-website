What Is the CAT Exam?

The Common Admission Test (CAT) is one of the most prestigious management entrance exams in India. Conducted annually by the Indian Institutes of Management (IIMs), it opens doors to elite MBA programmes. CAT is not just an exam — it is a gateway to a transformative career in business management, making it a top choice for ambitious graduates.

Every year, over 2.5 lakh students appear for CAT, competing fiercely for limited seats. CAT scores are accepted by premier institutions like IIMs, FMS Delhi, SPJIMR Mumbai, and many others. If you are dreaming of an MBA, the CAT exam is your golden ticket.

Eligibility Criteria for CAT Exam

1. Academic Requirements
To be eligible for the CAT exam, candidates must hold a bachelor's degree from a recognised university with a minimum of 50% aggregate marks (or 45% for SC/ST/PwD categories).

2. Age and Other Conditions
There is no upper age limit for the CAT exam, making it accessible to individuals of all ages. However, final-year undergraduate students can also apply, provided they complete their degree by the stipulated deadline.

Ensuring you meet these criteria is the first step toward your CAT journey.

Step-by-Step Guide to Registering for CAT:

Visit the official CAT website (iimcat.ac.in) and register with your email ID and phone number. Fill out the application form carefully with academic and personal details. Upload required documents including your photograph, signature, and category certificate (if applicable). Finally, pay the application fee online.

CAT Exam Pattern and Structure

CAT is a computer-based test (CBT) with a duration of 120 minutes. It comprises three sections. Each section has its own time limit and switching between sections during the exam is not allowed. Scoring involves +3 marks for correct answers and -1 mark for wrong answers in MCQs. Non-MCQ (TITA) questions have no negative marking.

CAT 2025 Preparation Strategy

Preparation is the backbone of CAT success. Here is how to go about it:

1
Effective Study Plan: Devote at least 4–6 hours daily for preparation. Focus on weak areas first while maintaining strengths. Divide your time between learning concepts and practising problems.
2
Start Early: With dedicated effort, 6–9 months is sufficient to prepare thoroughly. Starting early lets you cover the entire syllabus without last-minute stress.
3
Mock Tests — A Game Changer: Start with 1–2 mocks per week, increasing frequency as the exam nears. Analyse your mistakes and refine your approach for better scores. Mock analysis is more important than the mock itself.
4
Focus on Accuracy Over Attempts: CAT rewards selective attempts. Aim for 90%+ accuracy on attempted questions rather than attempting everything blindly.
5
Recommended Books: QA — "How to Prepare for Quantitative Aptitude for CAT" by Arun Sharma. VARC — "Word Power Made Easy" by Norman Lewis. DILR — "Logical Reasoning and Data Interpretation for the CAT" by Nishit K. Sinha.
Common Mistakes in CAT Preparation
Ignoring Mock AnalysisMock tests without thorough analysis don't help. Understand what went wrong and focus on weak areas.
Overlooking Non-MCQ (TITA) QuestionsSince these questions have no negative marking, they are scoring opportunities. Never leave them unanswered.
Neglecting VARCMany students focus heavily on Quant and DILR, sidelining VARC. A balanced approach is vital to excel in all sections.
Relying Solely on ShortcutsDon't rely only on shortcuts — understand the logic behind concepts. Overloading with too many resources can confuse rather than clarify.
What to Do on Exam Day

Last-Minute Preparation Tips: Revise key formulas, concepts, and strategies. Avoid learning anything new on the day before the exam. Get a good night's sleep to stay fresh and alert.

What to Carry to the Exam Centre:

Admit card (printed copy)
Valid photo ID — Aadhaar, PAN card, or Passport
Follow the dress code as per official guidelines
Do not carry watches, calculators, or mobile phones
Reach the exam centre at least 30 minutes early

Stay confident, trust your preparation, and approach the exam with a positive mindset.

After the Exam — What's Next?

Understanding the Percentile System: CAT results are presented in percentiles, not raw scores. A 99+ percentile generally guarantees a call from top IIMs, depending on other criteria like academics and work experience.

Steps After CAT Results: Prepare for Group Discussions (GD), Written Ability Tests (WAT), and Personal Interviews (PI). Keep documents ready for application processes. Research colleges and their selection criteria to prioritise your options. This phase is critical as it determines your entry into a top MBA programme.

Frequently Asked Questions — CAT 2025
What is a good score in the CAT exam?
A good CAT score is one that places you in the 99th percentile or above, typically translating to a score of 100–120+ depending on the year.
Is there negative marking in the CAT exam?
Yes, there is negative marking for MCQs. +3 marks are awarded for each correct answer and -1 mark is deducted for each incorrect MCQ answer. TITA (non-MCQ) questions have no negative marking.
Can I prepare for CAT without coaching?
Yes, many aspirants crack CAT through self-study using online resources, mock tests, and a disciplined schedule.
How many months are enough for CAT preparation?
With dedicated effort, 6–9 months is sufficient to prepare thoroughly for the CAT exam.
What are the fees for the CAT exam?
The application fee is ₹2,600 for General category and ₹1,300 for SC/ST/PwD candidates.
How many attempts can I make for the CAT exam?
There is no limit on the number of times you can appear for the CAT exam. You can take it annually as long as you meet the eligibility criteria.
Can I switch between sections during the CAT exam?
No, you cannot switch between sections during the CAT exam. Each section has a fixed time limit of 40 minutes and must be completed within that time frame.
